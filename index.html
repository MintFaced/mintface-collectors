Biography

MintFace is a cryptoartist and founder of The Line... a physical and digital home for tokenized art in New Zealand. Formed at the intersection of grief, fatherhood, and the arrival of the blockchain as a new kind of permanence, the name carries a single insistence: that connection is both subject and method.

The practice emerged in 2021 from the edge of the world, during a period of stillness that made the distance between people feel both more painful and more worth recording. What began as an attempt to leave a trail... coordinates, timestamps, inscriptions... became something larger: a decade-long corridor that the work keeps building.

MintFace refuses a signature look. The output moves between painting, photography, AI collaboration, and generative editions... not as versatility, but as insistence that no single medium is large enough to hold what needs saying.

Painting / AI / Augmented Reality

Hand-painted flowers worked to completion, then offered to AI as source material. The machine returns its vision — displayed on the physical canvas via augmented reality. It asks the only question that matters: who is the student, and who is the master?

Each physical painting becomes a trigger for the tokenized work to play. Collectors standing before the canvas experience both visions simultaneously, comparing and contrasting the two.

Patrimora

Generative Art — On-chain

Inspired by senbazuru — the Japanese tradition of folding 1,000 origami cranes. Named for a word MintFace coined: patrimora — a legacy delayed in its realization. A memorial to a son growing up without a father, through the motif of cranes.

Cranes are thought to represent long life. The name means an inheritance delayed by history, culture, or generation — something owed, not yet received.

Roads
& Rivers

Photography — Aerial — North Island, NZ

Roads are for travelling. Rivers go with the flow. When the two overlap, you get to decide which way you go. MintFace drove the North Island of New Zealand — post-pandemic, little traffic, the freedom of open roads — photographing bridges.

Seven of the 36 bridges were washed away in Cyclone Gabrielle, February 2023. The collection sparked conversations about memory, crossing, where people were going and why it mattered. Featured in the NZ Herald.

Geodetic
Home

Paint on Timber Sculpture — Cultural Object

A geodetic sculpture exhibited at the opening of The Line Gallery, May 2025. Visitors were invited to mark-make directly on its surface — signatures, drawings, names accumulated over weeks. The structure invited people to pause around it, as one does at a New Zealand summit marker.

In a time where algorithms divide, the geodetic sculpture brought people together on a single surface — and reminded us how to speak in person again.
